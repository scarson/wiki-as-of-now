## Problem Statement

Sam is a casual Wikipedia editor who wants to contribute more, and whose specific pet
peeve is out-of-date temporal claims — sentences that were future-tense when written
but whose date is now in the past ("the Pentagon is expected to award a contract in
2017"), especially in military procurement / defense-program articles.

The real, observed problem is **not** that these are hard to find or hard to research
individually. It is **throughput and flow-protection**: there are many of them, each
one pulls the reader out of their flow for 30–45 minutes of research-and-edit, it feels
overwhelming, so the net result is *zero* edits made. (See the demand evidence below.)

---

## What Makes This Worth Building (and the reframe)

The original spec's stated thesis (its "Core problem" section) is that "the hard part
is often not noticing staleness, but discovering what happened next." The office-hours
session established that for this editor that is only **half** true:

- Detection is essentially free for him — he spots these instantly.
- Per-item research is tractable — ~30 minutes for a hard one (the SBX-1 case below),
  5–10 minutes for a typical procurement item.
- **The binding constraint is volume × context-switch cost.** Many items, each
  breaking flow, equals overwhelm, equals nothing done.

So the product is a **batch-triage assistant that protects editing flow**, not a
research oracle. Its job is to drive the per-edit human cost down to roughly verify-and-
write (target under 5 minutes per edit, with research running in the background and
async), so a weekend session can clear a real batch.

The second thing that makes it worth building: done conscientiously, it is a credible
**build-in-public portfolio piece** — "an AI tool that respects Wikipedia's 2026 AI
rules" is a stronger, more interesting story than "AI writes my edits." The constraint
is the differentiator.

### Demand evidence (from the session)
- **Sea-Based X-Band Radar (SBX-1):** stale at "departed Pearl Harbor 26 September
  2019." Sam spent ~30 minutes researching its current status from a good-enough source
  and went to make the edit (blocked only by a corporate-VPN IP edit block — explicitly
  out of scope). Real behavior, not a hypothesis.
- **Military procurement articles** with past-due future tense, "often multiple per
  article," which he declines to tackle because doing them manually mid-read costs
  30–45 minutes and there are too many. This is the throughput problem stated in his
  own words.

## Constraints

- **The compliance social contract is sacrosanct and binding.** Every design and build
  choice is subordinate to the guardrails in `docs/policy/wikipedia-genai-compliance.md`.
  The LLM is a relevance classifier over real retrieved documents, never an author of
  article content and never a source; the human reads, verifies, writes the prose, and
  supplies citations; the tool emits native wikitext and never submits edits.
- Low fixed hosting cost; expensive (LLM) actions metered and gated.
- Open source; legible to humans and coding agents.
- Scratch-your-own-itch first; anonymous demo mode for portfolio; "thrilled if others
  use it" is a bonus, not a requirement.
- **Boil the lake:** the full v1 (the three milestones below) is built before Sam
  expects to use it; sequence by build dependency, not earliest personal utility.

## Premises (agreed in session)

1. The binding constraint is throughput / flow-protection, not detection or per-item
   research difficulty. The product is a batch-triage assistant.
2. v1 detector scope = future-tense/expectation claims tied to an explicit past year
   (date-anchored), tuned for precision over recall. Vaguer "currently / as of"
   detection is deferred.
3. The LLM is bounded to: normalize the hanging question into neutral queries;
   relevance-triage real retrieved documents; point at the resolving passage (verbatim
   quote, advisory support). It never originates facts or citations, never synthesizes
   across sources, never writes article prose.
4. The human always reads/verifies each source, writes the sentence, owns the edit.
   Tool output = per-claim evidence cards + mechanical `{{cite}}` skeletons from real
   metadata + a human-editable, mechanically-generated edit summary carrying an
   AI-assist disclosure.
5. Two modes: ad-hoc capture (drop a link into a queue) and a pageview-ranked batch
   queue within a bounded topic.
6. No full Wikipedia-dump pipeline for v1. Substitute a pageview-informed seed list
   within the domain (category/WikiProject × Pageviews API, via live MediaWiki) plus a
   small real fixture corpus for development and testing.
7. Output is native wikitext; the human pastes into Wikipedia's source editor and does
   final touch-up there. No in-tool re-implementation of Wikipedia's editor.

## Approaches Considered

Because the architecture is largely settled by the spec and the compliance contract,
the approaches differ in **scope and sequencing of the first build**, not in
fundamentally different designs.

### Approach A — Thinnest vertical slice
One article at a time, paste a URL, deterministic detector, research one candidate,
evidence cards, snippet assembler, copy wikitext. No queue, auth, corpus, or pageviews.
Effort: S–M. Risk: Low. Proves the compliance-shaped loop end to end fastest.

### Approach B — The batch workflow
A plus the two-mode queue (ad-hoc capture + pageview-ranked batch queue within the
military-procurement seed list), async research, Arctic Google OAuth + anonymous demo
mode + per-user quotas + research kill-switch. Effort: L. Risk: Medium. This is the
version that actually addresses the throughput problem.

### Approach C — Compliance-and-credibility-first (portfolio-forward)
A's loop plus the public trust story front and center: an About page rendered from the
compliance contract, the audit log + disclosure + "show your work" transparency as
first-class UI, build-in-public artifacts. Effort: M. Risk: Low–Medium. The showcase
layer.

## Recommended Approach

**Build all three as one integrated v1 (boil the lake), sequenced by build dependency.**
Since the tool will not be used until the whole thing is done, there is no value in
optimizing for earliest personal utility; there is value in a clean dependency order
that never builds on an unproven foundation.

**Stack decision (resolves the spec's open frontend question):** Next.js on Cloudflare
Workers + D1, reusing the proven pattern from `scarson/twin-cities-tee-times`
(Next.js + Wrangler + D1 + **Arctic** for edge-native Google OAuth). This trades the
spec's deferred React-Vite-vs-Next decision for "reuse what already works on this
exact platform," removing a spike and a class of deployment risk.

### Build sequence (dependency-ordered)

1. **Foundation.** Repo scaffold (Next.js on CF Workers, Wrangler, D1), config system,
   core domain types, the research **provider interface** (so Gemini is swappable), and
   D1 migrations — including the **foundational append-only audit/activity log** from
   commit one (the contract's audit-log guardrail makes this non-deferrable; it is also
   the backbone for disclosures, verification gating, and later engagement stats).
   **Async execution primitive (decided, not deferred):** Cloudflare Workers have no
   long-running process model, and the flagship "drop a URL → research runs in the
   background → return later" flow cannot exist without one. v1 uses **Cloudflare
   Queues** for research jobs (a producer enqueues on URL-drop; a consumer worker runs
   the research pipeline and writes results + audit entries). Cloudflare Workflows or
   Durable Objects are the alternatives if multi-step durability/retries outgrow Queues;
   pick at Foundation time. Verify whether the tee-times pattern already includes a
   background primitive — if not, treat this as net-new.
2. **Deterministic detector + fixtures.** Date-anchored stale-claim detector with an
   explainable score breakdown, developed TDD against a small real **fixture corpus**
   (SBX-1 plus a handful of procurement articles) and a hand-labeled gold set. Precision
   over recall; track false-positive rate. (No LLM — contract guardrail.)
   **Blocking dependency:** the precision target depends on having parse context to
   suppress false positives (historical quotations, already-resolved-nearby claims,
   completed-narrative dates — the spec's negative-pattern list). So the article-text
   representation (parsed, section-aware text plus the stale claim's original wikitext
   offset/span, so the detector has context and the assembler knows where the edit goes)
   must be decided and built *before* the detector, not parked. This is a real
   dependency of this milestone, not an open question.
3. **Single-article loop (Approach A).** Paste a URL → live MediaWiki fetch + revision
   validation → detect → candidate display → research one candidate through the provider
   interface (structured official-source retrieval and/or Gemini grounding behind the
   interface; verbatim-quote check; advisory support) → evidence cards → snippet
   assembler (plaintext sentence + mechanical wikitext `<ref>` tags) → mechanical
   disclosure + summary derivation → copy native wikitext.
   **Contract-critical constraint on any grounding path:** the verbatim-quote check
   (guardrail G8) and the untrusted-content separation (G15) both require the tool to
   hold the *real fetched page text*. So any provider path — including Gemini Google
   Search grounding — MUST result in the tool independently fetching the candidate URL's
   page text to run the deterministic verbatim check against. A grounding path that
   returns only model-summarized results without tool-fetched page text bypasses a
   sacrosanct guardrail and is not permitted. The provider interface must therefore
   yield resolvable URLs whose pages the tool fetches itself, not model-asserted answers.
   **Degradation states (the contract requires honesty):** the worksheet must clearly
   show "likely stale, but no strong current source found," "possible update, weak
   support," "research provider unavailable (cached/none)," and "article changed since
   detection" — never a false impression of resolution.
4. **Transparency surface (Approach C, part 1 — C is delivered across steps 4 and 7).**
   "Show your work" view: rankings, the non-selected candidate set, and the per-edit
   audit trail. Cheap once the audit log and cards exist; it is the portfolio
   differentiator and a contract requirement (show-your-work guardrail).
5. **Auth + anonymous mode + quotas (Approach B, part 1).** Arctic Google OAuth (Arctic
   is the edge-native OAuth library from the tee-times pattern), anonymous demo/browse
   mode, per-user quotas on research, and an admin kill-switch for the research layer.
   Quotas meter the expensive unit — research-job invocations per user per day (config-
   driven default) — per the spec's quota model. **Wikipedia-account linking** is a thin,
   *optional* v1 feature: an unverified self-declared username shown on the user's
   profile (low blast radius, like listing a LinkedIn URL on a GitHub profile) that also
   seeds the per-user attribution the deferred engagement stats will use. If it isn't
   wired to that seam, cut it from v1.
6. **Two-mode queue + seed list (Approach B, part 2).** Ad-hoc capture queue;
   pageview-ranked batch queue within the military-procurement seed list (category ×
   Wikimedia Pageviews API); async research jobs over Cloudflare Queues (drop URL →
   background research → return to a worksheet). **Async revision race:** because the
   article can change between detection/research time and worksheet-open time, the
   worksheet re-validates the article's current revision on open and flags any candidate
   whose source revision drifted or whose stale claim no longer exists (honoring the
   spec's live-validation invariant).
7. **Public About + transparency polish (Approach C, part 2).** About page rendered from
   the compliance contract; build-in-public artifacts; engagement/stats **data capture**
   as additive columns/views over the existing day-one audit log (not a second event
   pipeline), with the dashboard/streaks themselves deferred to v2 per
   `docs/design/future-features.md`, and gamifying quality-not-volume per that doc.

## How this reconciles with the main spec

The spec remains authoritative on architecture; this session changes or sharpens:

- **Core-problem thesis:** reframed from "the hard part is discovering what happened" to
  "the hard part is throughput across many items." Drives a batch-triage product and
  elevates the article-at-a-time + queue workflows over topic-browse.
- **No replacement prose (a spec non-goal):** hardened from a product choice into a
  compliance requirement, and extended — the research layer outputs **per-claim evidence
  cards**, never drafted sentences; the human assembles the edit.
- **LLM research layer:** scoped to relevance-classification over real retrieved
  documents with a verbatim-quote check and advisory-only support, per the contract —
  not an answer-and-cite oracle.
- **Corpus ingestion (spec data-source and pipeline sections):** deferred for v1 in
  favor of a pageview-informed seed list + fixture corpus via live APIs.
- **Frontend framework (spec's tracked open decision):** resolved to Next.js on
  Cloudflare Workers by reuse of the tee-times pattern.
- **Audit/activity log:** elevated to a foundational, day-one system (contract guardrail);
  it also serves observability and the deferred engagement stats.
- **New cross-cutting commitments** not in the original spec: the responsible-automated-
  access etiquette, the untrusted-fetched-content / prompt-injection handling, the
  copyright/close-paraphrasing rule, the incident-response posture, and the multi-user
  scope — all in the compliance contract.

## Blocking dependencies (must be decided before the milestone that consumes them)

- **Async execution primitive** → Foundation (step 1). Decided: Cloudflare Queues for
  research jobs; Workflows/Durable Objects if durability needs grow.
- **Article-text representation** → detector (step 2). Decided in principle: parsed,
  section-aware text plus the stale claim's original wikitext offset/span. Residual: the
  exact parser/library and how offsets survive into the snippet assembler.
- **Grounding must yield tool-fetched page text** → single-article loop (step 3), so the
  verbatim-quote check can run. Non-negotiable (sacrosanct guardrail).

## Open Questions (to track, not blocking)

- **Structured official-source connectors — scope v1 to one (or zero).** Each connector
  (defense.gov contract announcements as fragile HTML; SAM.gov and USAspending as APIs
  with registration; GAO as HTML) is its own normalization + metadata + rate-limit
  problem — roughly M-effort each, not S. v1 ships with at most **one** structured
  connector behind the provider interface, or zero (Gemini grounding with mandatory
  tool-side page fetch + verbatim check only); the rest are deferred. Don't treat "which
  first" as if they're cheap to add in bulk.
- **"Verified = opened source" enforcement.** Primary mechanism is an explicit "I opened
  and read this source" confirmation, because browser tab-focus tracking for a
  `target=_blank` open is unreliable and not worth instrumenting. The audit log records
  this honestly as a proxy for reading (per the contract's G5 proxy admission).
- **Citation metadata extraction depth:** how far the deterministic parser goes before
  falling back to human-supplied metadata.
- **Pageviews integration specifics:** ranking window, refresh cadence, and how it
  doubles as an impact stat later.

## Success Criteria

- Sam can clear a batch of date-anchored stale military-procurement claims in a single
  weekend session, at roughly under 5 minutes of human time per edit (research async),
  with every edit independently defensible on Wikipedia content-policy merits and
  carrying an AI-assist disclosure.
- Detector precision on the date-anchored class is high (low false-positive rate on the
  gold set) — the queue is trustworthy enough not to become its own chore.
- Every compliance guardrail is enforced or honestly flagged as unenforceable; the audit
  log is complete; the public About page renders the contract.
- Anonymous demo mode works as a portfolio showcase.

## Distribution Plan

It is a web service on Cloudflare Workers (existing-style deploy via Wrangler), so
distribution is deployment, not packaging. CI/CD via GitHub Actions → `wrangler deploy`
on merge; public OSS repo; permissive license per the spec. No binary/package
distribution needed.
